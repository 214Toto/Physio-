Correct Answers & Explanations

Q1: it binds to the molecule and changes shape during transport. A transporter mechanism involves the protein binding to the molecule and undergoing conformational changes to transport it across the membrane.


Q2: can only transport a molecule down a gradient. Passive transport proteins facilitate movement down a concentration gradient without energy input.


Q3: The cell's osmotic balance would be disrupted and the cell would begin to swell. Inhibition of Na-K ATPase disrupts ion balance, leading to water influx and swelling.


Q4: transport of one ion down its gradient provides the energy to transport another molecule against its gradient. Coupled transport uses the energy from one molecule moving down its gradient to power transport of another against its gradient.


Q5: opens in response to a specific stimulus. Gated channels open or close in response to specific stimuli like voltage changes or ligand binding.


Q6: being freely permeable to all substances Cell membranes are selectively permeable, not freely permeable to all substances.


Q7: They contain three fatty acids chains Phospholipids contain two fatty acid chains, not three (triacylglycerols have three).


Q8: hydrophobic The fatty acid tails form a hydrophobic core that excludes water.


Q9: membrane proteins were not uniform and did not form flattened sheets This discovery showed proteins were globular and asymmetrically distributed, contradicting the sandwich model.


Q10: Singer and Nicholson They proposed the fluid mosaic model in 1972.


Q11: It is the movement of lipids and integral proteins within the lipid bilayer The fluid mosaic model describes the dynamic nature of membrane components.


Q12: fluidity buffer Cholesterol maintains optimal membrane fluidity across temperature changes.


Q13: are amphipathic Integral proteins have both hydrophobic and hydrophilic regions, allowing them to span the membrane.


Q14: completely extends through the membrane Transmembrane proteins span the entire membrane, unlike other membrane proteins that may be only partially embedded.


Q15: bonding to integral proteins through noncovalent interactions Peripheral proteins are attached to membrane surfaces through noncovalent bonds with integral proteins or lipid head groups.


Q16: These proteins are threaded through the ER membrane into the ER lumen The asymmetric orientation results from how proteins are inserted into the ER membrane during synthesis.


Q17: made by ribosomes located on the rough endoplasmic reticulum Membrane-associated proteins are synthesized by RER ribosomes and processed through the endomembrane system.


Q18: The function of the membrane differs on the inside and outside of the cell Different protein compositions reflect distinct functional requirements at each membrane surface.


Q19: small hydrophobic molecules Membranes are most permeable to small, nonpolar molecules that can dissolve in the lipid bilayer.


Q20: oxygen Small nonpolar molecules like oxygen diffuse most easily through the lipid bilayer.


Q21: They respond to specific hormones Aquaporins are water channels that don't typically respond to hormonal regulation.


Q22: small nonpolar molecules; lipid bilayer of Small nonpolar molecules diffuse directly through the lipid bilayer.


Q23: osmosis Osmosis is a passive process that doesn't require cellular energy.


Q24: hypertonic When extracellular fluid has higher solute concentration, it's hypertonic relative to the cell.


Q25: lower; higher More solute means less free water and greater osmotic pressure.


Q26: have equal concentrations of solute but not water Isotonic solutions have equal solute concentrations but may differ in water concentration.


Q27: have serious, perhaps fatal consequences because the red blood cells could swell and burst Distilled water is hypotonic and would cause hemolysis.


Q28: undergo plasmolysis Plant cells lose water and shrink away from cell walls in hypertonic solutions.


Q29: a hypotonic medium Without cell walls, bacteria would burst in hypotonic environments.


Q30: hypertonic to fresh water but hypotonic to the salt solution The cells gained water in fresh water (hypertonic to it) and lost water in salt solution (hypotonic to it).


Q31: requires a transmembrane protein Facilitated diffusion uses transport proteins but doesn't require energy.


Q32: Both facilitated diffusion and carrier-mediated active transport These are the two forms of carrier-mediated transport.


Q33: the rapid and continuous intracellular formation of glucose phosphates Glucose phosphorylation maintains the concentration gradient.


Q34: the binding of glucose triggers a conformational change in the carrier protein This is characteristic of carrier-mediated transport.


Q35: active transport Sodium is moving against its concentration gradient.


Q36: It transports 3 sodium ions out of the cell in exchange for 2 potassium ions This is the standard stoichiometry of the Na+/K+ pump.


Q37: phosphorylate the pump protein to change its conformation ATP provides energy by phosphorylating the pump.


Q38: sodium ions are transported down their concentration gradient Sodium-glucose cotransport uses the sodium gradient to power glucose uptake.


Q39: The phagocytic vacuoles would not fuse with the lysosome Membrane fluidity is required for vesicle fusion during phagocytosis.


Q40: is engulfment of large particles by the cell Phagocytosis is the process of engulfing large particles or microorganisms.


Q41: brings about the selective uptake of materials by enclosing them in membranous vesicles Receptor-mediated endocytosis is selective and forms vesicles.


Q42: The Golgi apparatus The perinuclear clear zone (Hof) typically represents the Golgi apparatus location.


Q43: actin, tubulin Myosin moves along actin microfilaments, while kinesin moves along tubulin microtubules.


Q44: Acid phosphatases Primary lysosomes contain hydrolytic enzymes like acid phosphatase.
